What the tweets of America's most iconic presidents reveal about their leadership

Barack Hussein Obama II and Donald John Trump were very different leaders of the same nation.

Born of a white Kansas woman and black Kenyan man, Obama was raised in humble beginnings and immersed in a ‘multiplicity’ of cultures, having lived in various countries from a very young age. (Nelson, 2021)

On the other hand, Trump, a son of a New York real estate developer, was raised with rigour. He built an ambitious career that eventually led him to tycoonify his family’s business. (BBC, 2020)

Despite having walked such divergent paths to the White House, and led their nation in distinct ways, they share the title of 'Social Media president' due in large part to, their presence on Twitter.

In fact, Twitter has helped redefine the volumes of American politics, with both presidents using it as mouthpiece to engage with the greater American public.

If such is the case, does the way in which Obama and Trump present themselves on Twitter accurately reflect their reputations respectively, as the Unifier and Divider?

A note on leadership

Not all shows of leadership are identical. According to Razzetti (2018), one's approach to leadership demonstrates much about their priorities for the people.

Unite and Build

A unifier aims to unite through a common cause, by empowering people to achieve something "bigger than themselves in personally meaningful and socially beneficial ways".

As a unifier in the public eye, Obama was thought to demonstrate selfless devotion towards the greater good, despite being criticised for his stringent reform and commitment to change. (Scotti, 2016)

Divide and Conquer

A divider seeks to create a common enemy through an 'us vs them' approach. Through this deception, they spark anger and violence to "break groups into smaller, less powerful ones".

Trump was unpopular for his divisive approach to leadership, blaming foreign influence to bond Americans together in the short term. However, he was remembered for the way he disillusioned and disempowered people. (Smith, 2020)

These attitudes towards leadership have been defined historically, but continue to be as true today as ever. So why would a leadership stance be so important for a powerhouse nation as the US?

On diversity

In what Frey (2018) calls a ‘diversity explosion’, the US has seen significant growth in mixed ethnicity minorities due to "youthful immigration from Latin America, and Asia" which has been crucial to a shift in the ethnic landscape. (Lee, 2020)

In the past four decades, all of the United States (some states albeit slower than others), have seen positive growth in their diversity index.

The National Equity Atlas (2021) outlines that "diversity - in the context of inclusion - is a driver of innovation, business growth and economic progress."

Thus a president who can bring these differing groups together continues to be more important than ever before, to help cultivate a prosperous future for the country.

In light of such a diverse makeup how did Obama and Trump communicate to appeal to voters, if at all?

On sentiment

The way a president aligns with the people starts with their choice of language. President Obama and President Trump utilised social media as a means of extending their nationwide reach and driving engagement - an advantage that presidents who preceded them, simply did not have.

Applying a sentiment analysis to their Twitter activity over time generates some interesting insights about their diverging approaches to leadership.

Obama tweeted sparsely throughout his presidency, when compared to the leadup to his re-election, as an incumbent.

At the very most, Obama tweeted just shy of 500 times in September 2012, rallying online followers in his bid for a second term.

Despite tweeting more to generate traction though, Obama maintained a neutral sentiment and therefore sought consistency in his messaging to the public even when his title as a leader was on the line.

Trump reassured, "I’ll give [Twitter] up after I’m president," yet tweeted over 150 times a month throughout his first term. In the lead up to re-election, the number of tweets climbed to over 400 on a monthly basis.

While in office, Trump tended to tweet about the 'good news' of his government's doing, but at the tail end of his first, and only term, his language grew in aggression.

Following his defeat by then president-elect Joe Biden, Trump was accused of inciting an insurrection, dividing the American people between the 'Patriots' who voted for him, and those who didn't. (Twitter inc., 2021)

Interestingly, their sentiments on Twitter reflect the outcome of their re-election, and more importantly, their actions as leaders.

On pronouns

Apart from sentiment, the deliberate use of pronouns in these presidents' rhetoric such as "I, you we or they" can either "show affiliation or create distance". (Bramley, 2001)

Even in Obama and Trump's Twitter language, their use of pronouns accentuates their leadership styles.

Obama truly lives up to his 'unified' approach to leadership through inclusive language. His use of 'we' and other collective pronouns implies "shared identity and responsibilities", as though he was keeping people together. In a similar manner, when he says 'you', this brings the audience into the context of his message.

This is in comparison with President Trump whose most used pronouns were 'third person' terms like 'they' and 'them', which place the greatest distance between a speaker and the party in question. (Bramley, 2001)

Trump tweeted with a personal perspective, almost as much as he used collective pronouns in his language.

On foreign policy

How do the presidents talk about and treat foreign powers?

As presidents of the US, one might assume that both Obama and Trump are focused on discussing and raising problems that have to do with internal policies.

The data tells us otherwise, however.

Interestingly, President Obama tweeted about the US almost twice as frequently than Trump.

In fact, Obama tweeted about American topics over 10 times as much as he acknowledged his foreign counterparts. However, Trump posted 200 tweets more about international topics than internal affairs.

From a glance, Trump already appears to have strayed from the internal challenges of his country, while Obama stayed true to his own nation.

Mapping out the countries mentioned in their tweets, it shows just how widespread Trump's international perspective was, as opposed to Obama.

President Obama's tweets on foreign nations appear to consist of diplomatically necessary exchanges, such as in response to the 2010 Chilean earthquake:

"The American people send their deepest condolences to the Chilean people. The U.S. is ready to help in any way."

However, on more than one occasion, President Trump has been accused of divisive language against other nations.

"[Mexico] laughs at our dumb immigration laws. They must stop the big drug and people flows, or I will stop their cash cow, NAFTA. NEED WALL!"

It could be said that President Obama truly adopted a 'unifier' mindset, speaking on behalf of the people, for the benefit of the nation.

Completely polar to his predecessor, President Trump demonstrated a 'divider' attitude making personal attacks on foreign nations, not necessarily in the interest of the people as a whole, and thus could be seen as self-serving.

Of the many platforms from which presidents’ voices have resonated from, there hasn’t been any one more topical than that of Twitter. As we’ve learned, just from examining the tweets of two powerful leaders, their rhetoric and language speaks volumes about the kind of leader they were.

As social media becomes inherently critical to presidential campaigns, a candidate's presence in the digital space serves as a valuable hint to voters about what kind of president they may eventually become.
