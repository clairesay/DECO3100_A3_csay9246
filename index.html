What the tweets of America's most iconic presidents say about how they lead

Barack Hussein Obama II and Donald John Trump were very different leaders of the same nation.

Born of a white Kansas woman and black Kenyan man, Obama was raised in humble beginnings and immersed in a ‘multiplicity’ of cultures, having lived in various countries from a very young age. (Nelson, 2021) On the other hand, Trump, a son of a New York real estate developer, was raised with rigour and diligence. He led an ambitious career that eventually led him to tycoonify his family’s business. (BBC, 2020)

Both men have walked such divergent paths to the White House and in turn, America has followed under two vastly different leadership methods. There continues to be debate on which of these gentlemen has done a ‘better’ job at leading the world’s richest, and most powerful nation. Just 10 years ago, presidents would only have the chance to plead their case through well prepared speeches broadcast on mainstream media, and even then, it would have been hard to know what they were thinking.

But the advent of Twitter has somewhat levelled the playing field. It is one platform that has redefined the volumes of American politics, enabling asynchronous conversations that give the president and the people equal voice.

If such is the case, does the way in which Obama and Trump present themselves on Twitter accurately reflect their reputations respectively, as the Unifier and Divider?

A note on leadership

According to Razzetti (2018), history tells that a leader can lead in one of two ways:

Unite and Build

Unifiers hope to unite people through a common cause, by empowering people to achieve something 'bigger than themselves' in personally meaningful and socially beneficial ways.

Divide and Conquer

Through creating a common enemy via an 'us vs them' approach, a divider seeks to deceive and feed anger and violence to ‘break groups into smaller, less powerful ones’. This approach is known to work in the short term, but eventually creates disillusion and disempowers people.

These approaches have been used and defined historically but are as true today as ever. But why would a leadership stance be so important for a powerhouse nation as the US?

On diversity

Exploring the demographic landscape of America over modern history, we can see just how much the racial makeup of the country has changed.

In what Frey (2018) calls a ‘diversity explosion’, the US has seen significant growth in Hispanic, Asian and mixed ethnicity minorities, meaning a president who can bring these differing groups together is more important than ever before, for the future of the country.

Let’s see how as president, Obama and Trump spoke to empower their country on a united, or a divided front.

On promises

In their bid to appeal to voters as presidential candidates, Obama and Trump diverged in their approaches to marketing their envisioned plan as leader, to the people.

On the campaign trail, both candidates participated in traditional campaign rallies to drive engagement in states across the nation. On top of this, both also utilised social media to a vast extent, as a means of extending their nationwide reach - an advantage that presidents who preceded them, simply did not have.

Obama’s 2008 presidential campaign employed 100 people to help manage his digital presence alone. As the first presidential candidate to ‘go viral’, another factor that contributed to his success was the way he shaped his communications to be around his personality and family. As an incumbent four years later, Obama continued to engage with his audience, launching a Q&A on Twitter, and Reddit.

Kiss (2008) notes that “our subconscious is hard-wired to assume that faces we see regularly are our own friends”. With a consistent, friendly online presence coupled with constant televised appearances, Obama quickly became a popular figure who could bring together ‘all Americans’.

While Obama humanised his campaign, Trump ‘dehumanised’ through his social media presence. Trump ‘boosted his reach’ as a wildcard candidate by leveraging controversial topics and adopting unpopular stances at the expense of racial minorities such as the Hispanic and Muslim communities.

By ‘sparking debate’ and generating anger, negativity and hate, Trump sought a leadership that blamed a ‘shared’ common enemy, ‘foreign’ powers who were supposedly meddling in the great American dream and future.

On political corrrectness

After being inducted into the Oval office, both Obama and Trump continued to take vastly different stances, particularly on the hot button topic of political correctness. The term political correctness began with an intention to avoid language that has been used historically to discriminate, belittle or offend minorities, in particular. (Mora, 2021) This kind of language has grown to be a hot button issue in the US, and both presidents have taken divergent stances on the topic.

On racial epithets and derogatory comments, Obama (2016) acknowledges that “when you use words like that, you're reinforcing people feeling like they're outsiders, and less than other Americans.” (Obama, 2016). On foreign policy

How do the presidents talk about and treat foreign powers?

If we have a look at how President Obama and President Trump talk about America, against the world, it is no surprise that both appear to be inwardly focused. Comparing the frequency by which either of them said the same word, Obama says words like America and American much more than Trump while in office.

Now when we look at the individual tweets by President Obama, we can see how he spoke about other nations. Although he mentioned more about the US, any words used to describe foreign nations were pleasant and kind.

Comparing these with President Trump, we can see a significant shift in focus. All the words thrown at foreign entities were on the side of hostile rather than kind.

Of the many platforms from which presidents’ voices have resonated from, there hasn’t been any one more topical than that of Twitter. As we’ve learned, just from examining the tweets of two powerful leaders, their rhetoric and language speaks volumes about the kind of leader they were.

As social media becomes inherently critical to presidential campaigns, a candidate's presence in the digital space serves as a valuable hint to voters about what kind of president they may eventually become.
