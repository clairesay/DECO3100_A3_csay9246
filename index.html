What the tweets of America's most iconic presidents say about how they lead

Barack Hussein Obama II and Donald John Trump were very different leaders of the same nation.

Born of a white Kansas woman and black Kenyan man, Obama was raised in humble beginnings and immersed in a ‘multiplicity’ of cultures, having lived in various countries from a very young age. (Nelson, 2021) On the other hand, Trump, a son of a New York real estate developer, was raised with rigour. He built an ambitious career that eventually led him to tycoonify his family’s business. (BBC, 2020)

Both presidents have walked such divergent paths to the White House and in turn, America has followed under two vastly different leadership methods. There continues to be debate on which of these leaders has done a ‘better’ job at leading the world’s richest, and most powerful nation. Just 10 years ago, presidents would only have the chance to plead their case through well prepared speeches broadcast on mainstream media, and even then, it would have been hard to know what they were thinking.

But the advent of Twitter has somewhat levelled the playing field. It is one platform that has redefined the volumes of American politics, enabling asynchronous conversations that give the president and the people equal voice.

If such is the case, does the way in which Obama and Trump present themselves on Twitter accurately reflect their reputations respectively, as the Unifier and Divider?

A note on leadership

Not all shows of leadership are identical. According to Razzetti (2018), one's approach to leadership demonstrates much about their priorities for the people.

Unite and Build

A unifier aims to unite through a common cause, by empowering people to achieve something "bigger than themselves in personally meaningful and socially beneficial ways". (Razzetti, 2018)

It demonstrates selfless devotion towards a mission and the greater good, although stringent reform and long term commitment is necessary.

Divide and Conquer

By creating a common enemy through an 'us vs them' approach, a divider seeks to deceive, sparking anger and violence to "break groups into smaller, less powerful ones". (Razzetti, 2018)

This approach is known to keep people on the same agenda in the short term, but eventually creates disillusion and disempowers people.

These attitudes towards leadership have been defined historically, but continue to be as true today as ever. So why would a leadership stance be so important for a powerhouse nation as the US?

On diversity

Exploring the demographic landscape of America since the turn of the 21st century, we can see just how much the racial makeup of the country has changed.

In what Frey (2018) calls a ‘diversity explosion’, the US has seen significant growth in Hispanic, Asian and mixed ethnicity minorities.

Observing transformations in ethnic origins amongst the American people from the past 17 years alone, shows that all of the United States (some states albeit slower than others), have seen positive growth in their diversity index.

The National Equity Atlas (2021) outlines that "diversity - in the context of inclusion - is a driver of innovation, business growth and economic progress." Thus a president who can bring these differing groups together continues to be more important than ever before, to help cultivate a prosperous future for the country.

In light of such a diverse makeup just how did Obama and Trump appeal to the voters, if at all?

On twitter activity

In addition to traditional, live political rallies, both Obama and Trump as presidents, also utilised social media as a means of extending their nationwide reach and driving engagement - an advantage that presidents who preceded them, simply did not have.

Interestingly, Obama tweeted sparsely in his first presidential campaign. However, as an incumbent in his campaign for re-election, Obama began to tweet in record amounts.

At the very most, Obama tweeted 500 times in September of 2012, rallying online followers in his bid for a second term. The majority of the tweets spoke of what he had already done, and what he could do for the nation in the future.

Unlike Obama in his first election, Trump maintained a consistently high tweet rate in the lead up to voting season. Although he reassured the media, saying "Don’t worry, I’ll give it up after I’m president," he consistently tweeted upwards of 150 times a month throughout his first term. Seeking re-election, the number of tweets he made climbed to over 800 on a monthly basis.

Although Twitter seemed to play a bigger role in Trump's first election campaign than in Obama's, the platform was undeniably pivotal as they sought re-election.

In the lead up to re-elections, the topics of the tweets and they way they handled timely issues potentially reveal key focuses and their promises to voters.

On foreign policy

How do the presidents talk about and treat foreign powers?

As presidents of the US, one might assume that both Obama and Trump are focused on discussing and raising problems that have to do with internal policies.

The data tells us otherwise, however.

Interestingly, President Obama tweeted about the US more than twice as frequently as Trump.

Mapping out the countries mentioned in their tweets, it continues to show that Trump had a greater international focus as opposed to Obama.

Obama tweeted about American topics over 10 times as much as he acknowledged his foreign counterparts. However, Trump tweeted near equal amounts about both American and international topics. He also made 200 tweets more regarding foreign affairs than internal affairs.

From a glance, Trump already appears to have strayed from the internal challenges of his country, while Obama stayed true to his own nation.

A deep dive into the tweets, categorised by country and sentiment, reveals even more about their perspectives on America against the World.

President Obama's tweets on foreign nations appear to consist of mostly 'necessary' comments, such as "Welcoming Prime Minister Singh of India for the first state visit of this administration." and "The American people send their deepest condolences to the Chilean people. The U.S. is ready to help in any way." which seem to be diplomatically necessitated exchanges.

It could be said that President Obama truly adopted a 'unifier' mindset, speaking on behalf of the people, for the benefit of the nation.

Completely polar to his predecessor, President Trump demonstrated a 'divider' attitude making personal attacks on foreign nations, not necessarily in the interest of the people as a whole, and thus was self-serving.

Of the many platforms from which presidents’ voices have resonated from, there hasn’t been any one more topical than that of Twitter. As we’ve learned, just from examining the tweets of two powerful leaders, their rhetoric and language speaks volumes about the kind of leader they were.

As social media becomes inherently critical to presidential campaigns, a candidate's presence in the digital space serves as a valuable hint to voters about what kind of president they may eventually become.
